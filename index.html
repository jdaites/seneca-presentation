A quick intro

I’m Josh — I started InVintory straight out of university. I’m a software engineer by trade who fell in love with entrepreneurship early.

Before InVintory, my first startup was an app to help people skip bar lines. The concept was right — we just couldn’t execute. But I caught the bug.

Today

CEO & technical founder — I write code daily in Swift, TypeScript, and work across our entire stack.

Fun fact

The origin story

It started with my dad’s
wine collection

My dad is a serial entrepreneur. After exiting his previous venture — selling medical clinics to private equity — he just wanted to retire, write screenplays, and collect wine.

He tried Excel. He Googled. He found Cellar Tracker — the biggest player in the space for 20+ years.

It looked like Yellow Pages.

(Honestly, it still kind of does.)

🍷

The broken bottle that
started everything

My dad went looking for a specific bottle. Opened the fridge, rummaged through shelves — and a bottle rolled off and smashed on the ground. Red wine everywhere.

That was the bottle he was looking for.

That was the last straw. We had to build something better.

Mistakes
we made.

And there were a lot of them.

Mistake #1

Not trusting my gut

Early on, we spent a ton of investment dollars on things that didn’t work. Hosting events for a wine tech company sounds like a good idea — but it was a huge waste.

I knew in my gut it wasn’t the right strategy. But I didn’t push back hard enough.

When I finally took hold of marketing, I executed on what my instinct told me: we’re an app. Acquire users like apps acquire users.

THE SHIFT

Wine events & tastings

Sponsorships

→ Digital marketing campaigns
→ Content programs
→ App Store optimization
→ Understanding unit economics

Mistake #2

We hired too fast.
Then came the layoffs.

We raised money, got excited, built a team way too fast. Revenue wasn’t keeping up.

We had to do a layoff. It was one of the most painful things I’ve ever done.

💡 My advice: do not hire a single person until you can afford it. Don’t get investment dollars and immediately hire. Use that money for growth. Letting people go has long-term consequences.

I thought about quitting
more times than I can count.

Things weren’t working for a long time. We almost ran out of money. We did a layoff. Every challenge is a moment where you can choose to walk away.

The narrative in your head — combined with outside pressure — will constantly tell you to stop. If your gut believes in the idea, that’s the only signal that matters.

Rewrite the narrative.

What worked
for us.

Think outside the box

We raised from our users.
Nobody does that.

My dad suggested we email our user base instead of going to VCs. I thought he was crazy. We did it anyway.

$10M
pledged from one email

One email to our users. $10 million in pledges. I never would have tried it if someone hadn’t pushed me.

The playbook doesn’t always apply to your business. Don’t be afraid to try something that sounds ridiculous.

Know your numbers

Understand your
unit economics

Early on, understand what you’re pricing your product at. Based on that price, figure out what you need to acquire a customer for.

If you’re spending $1,000 to acquire a $10 customer, it doesn’t make sense. Don’t pursue that.

It won’t be perfect on day one. You’ll do things by hand that aren’t scalable. That’s okay. But have a plan.

✓ THIS MAKES SENSE

Spend $30 → Acquire a $120/yr subscriber

✗ THIS DOES NOT

Spend $1,000 → Acquire a $10 customer

The most important question

Understand why
you’re building.

VC funding looks glamorous. The press releases, the LinkedIn posts — you want to be one of those people. I was there.

Then I had a child. I realized I care about making money and providing for my family — not recognition. VCs only help businesses that need massive capital to reach massive valuations. We weren’t one.

Building is free.
Selling is not.

With AI tools, building software is virtually free now. That’s amazing — everyone should feel empowered to create.

But since building is free, the hard part — distribution, marketing, and sales — is what separates businesses from side projects. That still costs money, time, and skill.

A word of caution

Apps are becoming a
commodity.

Utilities and productivity apps are popping up everywhere. We’re headed into an era where people will build apps on the fly when they need them.

So find a reason why yours has staying power. Find your moat.

Data

The more people use it, the more valuable it becomes. Hard to replicate, impossible to rebuild overnight.

Community

People don’t leave communities. Build something people belong to, not just something they use.

Switching cost

Make your product so embedded in their workflow that leaving isn’t worth the effort.

A hot take

A salary alone
won’t set you free.

Unless you’re investing every dollar you earn in appreciating assets, you likely can’t achieve the financial freedom most people dream of through a traditional career alone.

Building a business is hard and messy. But it’s one of the only paths to uncapped earning potential.

Just start.

Sit down. Build something. Start to finish. Don’t spend time perfecting it.

Release the thing that’s not perfect. Get feedback. Get revenue. Fail. Have people tell you it sucks. Iterate. Continue.

It’s not the end of the world if your product sucks on day one. It’s not even the end of the world if it sucks in year one.

You have no excuse now but to get started.
